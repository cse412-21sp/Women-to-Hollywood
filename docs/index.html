Introduction

The motivation of this project is the underrepresentation of females within the American entertainment industry. Our goal is to find correlations between female presence in the cast or crew of movies, crew and the gross profit, ratings, and overall success of the media. In order to accomplish our goal, we’ve designed visualizations that translate the insights we found by analyzing various data sets into a format that is easier to understand and consume. Visualizations are, all in all, a better way of telling the story of womens’ presence within the film industry, and we hope that people will become more aware of the disparities that may exist.



Numbers in History

Do Female Directors Make More Movies Over Time?

The earliest movie available in IMDb is “Miss Jerry” was directed by Alexander Black in 1894. This is a black-and-white silent pre-film “Picture Play”, accompanied by narration and music, making it the first example of a feature-length dramatic fiction on screen. However, it is arguably considered “La Fée aux Choux” directed by French female director Alice Guy-Blaché is the world’s first narrative movie, and the first movie directed by a woman. Unfortunately this movie is not collected in IMDb database and the first movie directed by women was twenty years later in 1915, according to IMDb.

The development of movies industry was slow until it came to the sound era. The number of movies increased a lot after 1920s. However, there was not much growth of the movies directed by female. For a longtime from 1920s to 1960s, there were only under 10 movies published every year directed by women.

Starting from 1970s, with the growth of the entire movie industry, more and more movies are produced every year. The amount of movies directed by females has increased a lot since then, but it’s still much less than the number of male directed movies. Is this situation getting improved?

According to the ”% of movies directed by females”, starting from 1980s, women directors have made more and more contributions to the movie industry. Not only the total number of movies directed by females increased a lot, but also the ratio of these movies when comparing to men-directed-movies.



Women can’t direct Action? Gender Stereotypes in Directing and Movie Genres

Gender stereotypes exist everywhere, with no exception in the field of directing. For example, many people believe that women have better verbal abilities than men, whereas men are assumed to have better senses of action, adventure, and suspenseful movies. This stereotype may lower the chances of a female director getting a job in some programs. Guess which genre has the highest and lowest percentage of female directors? Interact with the visualization by selecting different genres and sees the answer.

The result has no surprise. Drama, Comedy, and Family movies are the top3 genres directed by a female. While action, adventure, and horror movies have a relatively low number. Some specific genres, for example, sports and western movies, though the number of data not as much as others, we can hardly see any these movies directed by females.



Do Movies Directed by Females Get Higher Ratings?

How Do the Ratings change over time?

The below visualization shows the average rating of all the movies publish each year by female and male directors. Although the ratings for movies directed by both female and male directors dropped since the World War II, the overall vote of movies directed by females are getting higher than those directed by males over the years. This trend is supported by large amount of movies produced after 1980s


The rating data for women-directed-movies before 1970s are sparse because of the limited number of movies. The statistical analysis after 1980s is more convincing, which shows significant difference in average ratings directed by females and males.



Do movies that pass the Bechdel Test receive different audience/critic ratings compared to movies that do not pass the test?

The Bechdel Test is a measure of the representation of women in fiction. It asks whether a work features at least two women who talk to each other about something other than a man. Using this measure, we hope to find a discrepancy between the ratings received by the movies that pass the test compared to those movies that do not pass the test.


From the main scatter plot we cannot tell much as the two groups seem to be distributed pretty evenly throughout the graph. By selecting which group we would like to analyze (movies that passed or movies that failed the test) in the interactive visualization and looking at the marginal density histograms, we can find discrepancies. For example, when we look at the variable marginal density plot for x (critics ratings), we see that movies that failed the Bechdel test receive high critics ratings. When we look at the density plot for y (audience ratings) we can see that movies that passed the Bechdel Test tended to get a lot of audience ratings around 0.6, while movies that do not pass the test, get more ratings around 0.8.

It appears that, out of the movies that failed the Bechdel Test, a higher percentage of them received high critic and audience ratings, compared to the percentage of movies that passed the Bechdel test. It is worthy to take note, that only 18 movies out of 50, did in fact pass the test. Both groups have movies all over the scatterplot without any clustering occurring. I would conclude that there is not enough statistical evidence to show that the Bechdel test has any signifiant association to movie ratings.




Are budget and box office earnings connected to presence of females in the cast?

The interactive plot below includes data on the cast of the 50 top-grossing movies of 2016, compared to either production budget or domestic box office earnings on opening weekend. Movies can be highlighted on whether or not they pass some condition on casting and billing of female actors, in addition to the Bechdel test.


For movies that had the highest box office earnings on opening weekend (over $100M), the percentage of overall cast that was female was less than 35%. Judging by the titles of these movies, most of these high-earners with a smaller female cast were action/superhero and adventure movies. For films with an overall female cast of 45% or above, the production budget and box office openings appeared to be lower. Movies with a female actor in the first billing spot tended to have a higher percentage of overall female cast. Films that passed the Bechdel test also tended to have a higher percentage of women in the overall cast.



How does the female to male ratio of the crew compare with the success of the movie?

The visualization below investigates the ratings of movies versus the ratio of female to male cast and crew members in movies. Users are able to toggle and compare the Rotten Tomatoes scores from critics, audiences, and also IMDB scores of movies.


The data for the visualization was based off of the counts of guesses of a cast or crew member’s gender for different movies and it is important to note that the full cast and crew was not listed for the movies so the ratios may not be completely accurate. Just from looking at the distributions of the ratings versus the ratio of females to males, there is no strong correlation that indicates a film will be more or less successful with the a higher presence of females. The lack of a correlation may then serve to support the idea that having more female representation within the cast and crew will have no harm towards the production.

Conclusion:

In summary, after analyzing various metrics regarding women in the movie industry, we found the following insights:

  • More and more movies are produced by female directors over time, not only the absolute number but also the ratio of these movies when comparing the male-directed movies. However, women-directed-movies are still less than 20% of the total by 2020.
  • Even though female directors increase over time, gender stereotypes over genre still exist. Female directors’ number is significantly less than male in stereotypical “males” movies like action and adventure.
  • It’s interesting that ratings for movies directed by both female and male directors show a decreasing trend. However, the overall ratings of movies directed by females are higher than those directed by males.
  • Films that had a higher percentage of female cast overall tended to have both a lower production budget as well as lower box office earnings on opening weekend.
  • When comparing the ratio of females to males in the cast and crew of various movies versus the ratings, we found that there wasn’t much of a correlation. It was not necessary that a movie with a higher ratio was more or less successful.

The analysis that we conducted demonstrated that women are not well-represented, but are slowly achieving representation. It would be interesting to see a larger dataset inclusive of more movies per year. It would be interesting to visit this project again in the future to note any improvements with women in hollywood.
